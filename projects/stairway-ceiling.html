Nobody looks up — how I transformed my stairway ceiling from forgotten to unforgettable

Marcie  ·  September 2024

I want to tell you something that took me a long time to figure out: the ceiling is the most underrated canvas in your entire home. Nobody looks up. Which is exactly why, when you do something beautiful up there, it stops people cold.

This stairway ceiling project lived in my head for over a year before I touched it. The original ceiling had a heavy popcorn texture that felt dated and oppressive. Every time I walked up those stairs I thought this has so much potential — and then I'd go make dinner and forget about it for another three months.

"The ceiling is the most underrated canvas in your entire home. Nobody looks up — which is exactly why, when you do something beautiful up there, it stops people cold."

Step one: scraping the texture

The first thing I did was lightly mist the ceiling with water using a spray bottle. This is the key step most tutorials gloss over — you want the texture damp, not soaked. Too dry and you're fighting it. Too wet and you risk damaging the drywall underneath.

I used a wide drywall scraper and worked in sections. It came off in satisfying sheets once I found the right pressure. My arm was sore for three days. Worth every second.

Pro tip

Lay down a plastic drop cloth before you start. Scraped texture is basically fine drywall dust and it gets everywhere. Change clothes before you come back inside.

After scraping, I let the ceiling dry completely — a full 24 hours — before doing anything else. Then I lightly sanded any rough patches and applied a skim coat of joint compound to get a smooth, even surface.

The design process (attempts one, two, and finally three)

I tried three different approaches before landing on what you see in the photos. The first was a simple board and batten pattern — too plain for a slanted ceiling. The second was a diamond lattice — too busy on the diagonal angle. The third, a geometric coffered panel system, was the one. It worked with the angle instead of fighting it.

I sketched the layout on paper first, then taped it out on the actual ceiling with painter's tape before committing. That extra step saved me hours of regret.

What I used

  • 1×4 MDF boards (cut to fit)
  • Liquid Nails construction adhesive
  • Brad nailer + 2" brad nails
  • Joint compound for seams
  • Sherwin-Williams Alabaster (base coat)
  • Sherwin-Williams Creamy (trim coat)
  • Painter's tape — a lot of it

The two-tone decision

This is the detail that makes the whole thing. I painted the field of each panel in Sherwin-Williams Alabaster, and the molding trim in Creamy — a shade just a touch warmer. In photographs it reads as barely-there. In person, the depth it creates is everything.

The finished ceiling — two tones, geometric coffering, entirely hand-built.

Was it worth it?

Every single time I walk up these stairs I catch myself looking up and smiling. That's the answer.

If you're eyeing a ceiling project and you've been talking yourself out of it — don't. Start with the tape. Sketch the design. The hardest step is deciding to begin.

Your homework

Walk through your house today and look up in every room. Find the one that has the most potential. That's your next project.
